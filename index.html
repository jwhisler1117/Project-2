Project 2 Persuasive or Deceptive Visualization?

Joe Whisler — jwhisler@ucsd.edu

Proposition

Complaints against the NYPD disproportionately involve Black complainants.

FOR the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Used a warm red color palette to evoke urgency and highlight higher counts of complaints.
  • Grouped years into 2-year blocks to smooth variation and emphasize persistent disparity.
  • Alphabetical ordering and a dominant dark-red band visually direct attention toward Black complainants.

AGAINST the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Used soft blues and grays to create a neutral and analytical appearance, reducing emotional bias.
  • Limited the range to 2010–2019 to focus on recent progress and imply ongoing improvement.
  • Expressed data as a ratio rather than raw counts to normalize population differences and lower perceived disparity.

Final Reflection

These two visualizations demonstrate how framing and design choices can heavily influence interpretation. The heatmap emphasizes persistent racial disparities through color intensity and historical range, leading viewers to infer systemic bias. In contrast, the line chart reframes the same topic to suggest improvement and progress, using normalization and restrained colors to soften emotional impact.

This exercise reinforced how seemingly objective data can be visually shaped to convey distinct — even opposing — narratives. Context, color, and normalization can dramatically shift perception. Effective visualization requires balancing clarity and persuasion, ensuring visual design supports truth rather than distortion.
