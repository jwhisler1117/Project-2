Project 2 Persuasive or Deceptive Visualization?

Joe Whisler — jwhisler@ucsd.edu

Proposition

Complaints against the NYPD disproportionately involve Black complainants suggesting racial profiling.

FOR the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Used a red color palette to evoke urgency and highlight higher counts of complaints.
  • Grouped years into 2-year blocks to reduce clutter especially along the x-axis. Also reduced the number of groups by combining 'Unknown' and 'Not Reported' into 'Unknown' and also 'Refused' and 'Declined to Answer' into a single 'Refused'
  • Dominant dark-red band visually directs attention toward Black complainants.
  • -0.5 Score (slightly deceptive) as noted the dark-red band for Black complainants intentionally dominates the plot. Other racial groups complaint increases can be seen but visually much more subtle.

AGAINST the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Used a soft blue line to create a neutral and analytical appearance, reducing emotional bias.
  • Limited the range to 2010–2019 to focus on recent progress and imply ongoing improvement.
  • Transformed the data into ratios using Black complaints versus all other groups to show a lower perceived disparity.
  • -1.0 Score (deceptive) as this plot only chooses data from 2010-2019 where a overall reducted of % of Black complainants can be seen. The plots being in ratio form may also deceive a viewer to think Black complaints are going down when in reality the raw count of Black complaints has remained relatively consistent over the decade but the number of overall complaints has increased.

Final Reflection

Working on this project showed me how much data transformations and design choices can change the story a chart tells. For the FOR the Proposition heatmap, I grouped complaints into two-year periods and cleaned up the ethnicity labels so the data was more consistent. I also filled missing values with “Unknown.” Those steps made the chart less overwhelming but still kept the focus on showing how Black complainants dominate the overall number of complaints. For the AGAINST the Proposition line chart, I filtered the years down to 2010–2019 and calculated a new ratio comparing Black complainants to everyone else. That made the trend look clearer, but it also framed the data in a way that could make the disparities seem smaller than they really are.

The two visualizations tell opposite stories even though they come from the same dataset. The heatmap uses red tones and total counts to draw attention to how consistently high the number of Black complainants has been, suggesting possible racial bias. The line chart uses softer blues and a limited range to show what looks like progress. Neither one is “wrong,” but they push the viewer to focus on different parts of the truth. I didn’t realize before just how much power color, scale, and framing have to shape what people believe they’re seeing.

This assignment made me rethink what “ethical visualization” means. To me now, it’s about being transparent about the choices you make — what data you include, how you transform it, and what context you leave out. It’s fine to make persuasive visuals if you’re honest about how they were built and what they really show. It crosses the line when design choices hide important context or make something look more extreme (or more positive) than the data supports. The hard part isn’t making a chart that looks good — it’s making one that tells the truth without pretending to be neutral.
